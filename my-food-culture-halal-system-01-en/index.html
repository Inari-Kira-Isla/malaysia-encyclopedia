JAKIM Halal Certification: Malaysia's Global Leading Food Regulatory System

About JAKIM: Malaysia Islamic Development Authority

JAKIM (Jabatan Kemajuan Islam Malaysia, the Malaysia Islamic Development Authority) is the core regulatory body responsible for Islamic affairs under the Malaysian government. Established in 1997, it currently falls under the Prime Minister's Department. According to 2023 data, JAKIM has over 2,200 employees nationwide, with branch offices in every state, forming a comprehensive certification network across Malaysia.

As the world's first national-level system for systematically promoting halal certification, JAKIM's core functions include halal food certification, Islamic finance standard-setting, quality control of religious products, and development planning for Muslim communities. As of 2024, JAKIM processes over 50,000 halal certification applications annually from both domestic and international markets, making it the most influential Islamic standards body in Southeast Asia.

JAKIM uses "MyHijau" as its halal certification mark. All certified products must display this label on their packaging to enter the Malaysian market. The certification process covers eight major areas, including raw material sourcing, production environment, equipment cleaning, and personnel training, with an audit cycle typically taking 60 to 90 working days. Companies can apply online through JAKIM's official website or engage accredited third-party audit firms to assist with documentation.

Notably, JAKIM has signed mutual recognition agreements for halal certification with 34 countries, including Indonesia, Singapore, Brunei, and several Middle Eastern countries. Products certified by JAKIM can typically obtain market access quickly in these regions, providing significant strategic value for export-oriented businesses.

For food companies planning to enter the Malaysian market, it is recommended to prioritize understanding JAKIM's Halal Certification Manual and engage consultants familiar with Malaysian regulations to facilitate the process. For more detailed information on halal certification, please refer to the subsequent sections of this special report.

Halal Certification Process: From Application to Approval

According to 2023 official JAKIM data, the average certification processing time is 45 to 60 working days; however, the complete process actually encompasses multiple stages. Enterprises must first confirm compliance with the Malaysian Halal Standards (MS 1500:2019) before proceeding to the formal application stage.

Pre-Application Preparation Stage

Before submitting an application, enterprises must prepare a comprehensive Halal management manual covering four major areas: raw material sourcing, production processes, equipment cleaning, and staff training. According to JAKIM statistics, approximately 35% of applications are rejected due to incomplete documentation or insufficient raw material traceability information. It is recommended that enterprises begin preparation three months in advance and consult with JAKIM-recognized consulting firms.

Formal Application and Document Review

Applicants may submit their applications through the JAKIM official portal My Halal Portal, or visit any state branch office in person. After submission, JAKIM officers will conduct a document review period of approximately two weeks, focusing on verifying whether raw materials are sourced from Muslim-certified suppliers and whether production lines are separated from non-Halal products. Upon qualification, the application proceeds to the on-site audit stage.

On-site Audit and Product Testing

On-site audits are conducted by JAKIM-trained qualified Islamic enforcement officers, who examine factory conditions, cleaning procedures, and workers' knowledge of religious rituals. Certain products (such as processed foods) require additional samples to be sent to designated laboratories for ingredient analysis. 2022 data shows that the audit pass rate is approximately 78%.

Certificate Issuance and Ongoing Supervision

After passing all reviews, enterprises will receive a Halal certification certificate valid for two years, with renewal required six months prior to expiration. JAKIM implements surprise inspection mechanisms, randomly auditing approximately 20% of certified enterprises annually. Those who fail will have their certifications revoked and published on the official website.

  • Recommendation: First-time applicants may consider engaging JAKIM-recognized third-party certification bodies (HAB) to handle the process on their behalf. For example, services provided by the Selangor State Islamic Department can effectively reduce the risk of rejection.
  • Fee Reference: Application fees range from approximately RM500 to RM2,000, depending on product category and production scale.

Japanese Cuisine and Halal Certification: The Revolution After 2016

2016 is regarded as a watershed year for Japanese cuisine's expansion into the halal market. Prior to this, only individual Japanese food and beverage companies had applied for certification; however, since then, the annual growth rate of applications has reached 45% (JAKIM 2022 Annual Report). This shift was driven by rising demand for Japanese cuisine in Indonesia and the Middle East markets, while the Malaysian government launched the "Halal Industry Development Blueprint," streamlining the certification process for foreign companies and enabling Japanese food enterprises to enter the Muslim market at relatively lower costs.

The core challenge in halal adaptation of Japanese cuisine lies in ingredient substitution. Traditionally, ramen broth is often simmered from pork bones, and sushi rice vinegar may contain alcoholic seasoning—these ingredients do not comply with halal standards. Since 2016, Japanese chain restaurants in Vietnam and Indonesia have begun using chicken bone broth instead of pork bone broth, and rice vinegar instead of sake, successfully passing certification. According to the Malaysia Halal Development Corporation, approximately 120 Japanese restaurants had obtained local halal certification as of 2023, including Yoshinoya Malaysia outlets in Kuala Lumpur, and Southeast Asian locations of the Tokyo-based Sushi Atori brand.

For Japanese food and beverage companies looking to enter the halal market, a three-step approach is recommended: first, conduct an internal ingredient audit to identify all non-halal components; second, contact a JAKIM-recognized third-party audit firm for pre-assessment; finally, submit a formal application and address any non-compliant items. The typical certification cycle ranges from 6 to 9 months, with fees varying according to company size, generally between 5,000 to 15,000 ringgit. A notable success case is Kikkoman, the well-known Japanese soy sauce manufacturer, whose Malaysian production line received halal certification in 2019 and now supplies to supermarkets and food service channels across the entire ASEAN region.

In summary, the integration of Japanese cuisine and halal certification represents a new trend where tradition meets compliance. For food and beverage brands seeking to expand into the global Muslim market, obtaining certification is not merely a regulatory requirement but a key to unlocking the US$2.3 trillion halal market. To learn more about the halal certification application process, refer to the Malaysia External Trade Development Corporation (MATRADE)'s Guide to Halal Certification for Foreign Companies.

How to Identify Halal-Certified and Non-Halal Restaurants

As of 2023, more than 8,000 restaurants in Malaysia have obtained JAKIM halal certification; however, identification requires attention to several key indicators.

The primary identification method is to look for the JAKIM halal logo. Legally certified restaurants must display the blue JAKIM halal logo at the entrance or cash register. The certification number, in the format "JAKIM/FSSC 22000-XXXX:XXXX", is printed below the logo. Consumers can verify the certification validity by entering the number on the JAKIM official website—this method has a 97% accuracy rate. It is worth noting that some restaurants only have certification at specific branches rather than all locations, so it is still necessary to confirm before ordering.

In addition to visual signage, menu labeling is also an important reference. Certified restaurants typically display the halal logo on the first page of the menu or indicate "Halal Certified". If a restaurant cannot provide certification documents or speaks vaguely about it, Muslim diners should be extra cautious. According to a 2023 survey of Islamic consumers, approximately 68% of respondents proactively inquire about a restaurant's certification status.

Regarding practical recommendations, when dining at large shopping centers, priority can be given to certified restaurants within food courts, as food court management regularly reviews tenant qualifications. Abroad, one can rely on lists recognized by local Islamic organizations, such as MUIS certification in Singapore or BPJPH certification in Indonesia.

For tourists or first-time visitors, downloading the "MyHarapan" mobile application is recommended. It includes a nationwide database of certified restaurants, with results filterable by location and cuisine type, enhancing dining efficiency and peace of mind.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q1: What is JAKIM Certification? How can I quickly verify if a restaurant has been certified?

JAKIM stands for the "Department of Islamic Development Malaysia" (Jabatan Kemajuan Islam Malaysia), which manages the national halal certification system. The most direct way to verify is to look for the blue JAKIM halal logo at the restaurant entrance or above the cash register. The certification number, formatted as "JAKIM/FSSC 22000-XXXX:XXXX", is printed below the logo. Consumers can enter this number on the JAKIM official website to verify the certification validity period. This method has 97% accuracy.

Q2: Does the absence of a JAKIM logo necessarily mean a restaurant is not halal?

Not necessarily. Some restaurants may serve food that complies with Islamic dietary laws but have not yet applied for formal certification. However, Muslim consumers at such establishments often cannot verify whether the ingredient sources and preparation methods meet halal standards. It is recommended to prioritize restaurants displaying the JAKIM logo to ensure dietary compliance.

Q3: How long is JAKIM certification valid? Can a restaurant continue using the logo after expiration?

Standard JAKIM certification is valid for two years, after which the restaurant must reapply for review. Continuing to display the logo after certification has expired constitutes a violation and may result in fines or even revocation of certification. Consumers should verify the certification validity period when checking.

Q4: Does JAKIM certification only apply to restaurants? How can I verify supermarket food products?

JAKIM certification covers a wide range, including restaurants, food processing industries, and packaged foods in supermarkets. When purchasing packaged foods, consumers should look for the JAKIM halal logo on the packaging, or scan the certification QR code on the package to query product information. Major Malaysian supermarkets such as Malayan Banking Berhad (Maybank) chain supermarkets and Cold Storage all have dedicated halal food sections.

Q5: Are imported products with foreign halal certifications recognized in Malaysia?

Malaysia recognizes product certifications from certain foreign halal certification bodies, including Singapore's , Indonesia's , and Middle Eastern countries. However, restaurants using imported ingredients still require JAKIM approval to legally claim themselves as halal establishments in the country.
